<Callout title="Core idea">
  Strong hooks do not just create curiosity. They establish the right expectation for the proof that follows.
</Callout>

## Clarity beats cleverness in the opening seconds

A lot of weak hooks try too hard to sound surprising. The result is that the viewer notices the energy but does not understand the point. In short-form video, confusion is expensive.

The best hook usually gives the viewer a reason to keep watching in one clean thought. That thought can be a problem, a claim, a contrast, or a payoff preview.

## The hook should earn the proof section

The opening line should not be judged in isolation. A hook is only strong if it sets up the next section well. If the first line promises one thing and the proof section drifts somewhere else, retention weakens quickly.

That is why teams should review hooks in context instead of collecting isolated swipe examples.

## Rewrite hooks against the real structure

When teams rewrite hooks from scratch without reviewing the rest of the video, they often optimize the wrong thing. A better approach is to inspect the existing structure, identify where the drop begins, and rewrite the opener to hand off more cleanly into proof.
