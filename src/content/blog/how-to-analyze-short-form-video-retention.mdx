## Start with the first decision point

Most teams say they care about retention, but they review videos too loosely to act on what they see. The first useful question is not whether a video performed well overall. It is where attention started to leak and what the viewer likely needed at that exact moment.

For short-form content, the opening seconds carry disproportionate weight. If the hook is unclear, overlong, or detached from the eventual payoff, later optimization work does not matter much.

## Separate hook, proof, and close

A useful retention review breaks the video into operating sections. The hook wins permission. The proof section earns belief. The CTA close converts attention into the next action. When teams collapse all of that into one vague performance score, they lose the ability to improve the next creative.

This is why structured analysis matters more than a generic watch-time number. You need to know which section held, which section drifted, and which transition weakened momentum.

## Use the analysis to produce the next asset

Retention analysis only matters if it changes the next script, scene plan, or edit. The goal is not to produce commentary. The goal is to create a cleaner input for the next video.

That is where a system like Sigmora is more useful than manual note taking. The insight can move directly into an execution asset instead of staying trapped in a spreadsheet or review doc.
